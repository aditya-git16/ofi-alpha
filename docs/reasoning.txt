"At time t, given what I can observe about the order book, 
can I predict where the mid price will be in the future?"

Features are what you observe at time t. Labels are what actually happened after t. Every feature and label choice flows from that one question.

-- features --

for start we observe that we have tob data
we can derive mid price and spread from it -> these are Features

mid price -> because this is what we are trying to predict movement of 
spread -> ofi behaves differently depending on spread

we then have OFI itself 
sum of e_n over last n events (10, 50 , 100) -> multiple horizons to see which one has better predictive power

order book imbalance is another feature we will use for baseline comprison
obi = (bid_qty - ask_qty) / (bid_qty + ask_qty)

microprice (weighted mid) -> microprice = (ask_px * bid_qty + bid_px * ask_qty) / (bid_qty + ask_qty)

depth -> This comes directly from the Cont paper.
Their key result is that the OFI slope beta is inversely related to depth.
Deep markets absorb flow without moving. Thin markets move a lot per unit of OFI.

--- labels ----

Event-horizon labels
y_mid_10  = mid[t + 10 events] - mid[t]
y_mid_50  = mid[t + 50 events] - mid[t]
y_mid_100 = mid[t + 100 events] - mid[t]
Why event-horizon: This is how the Cont paper defines it. 10 events means 10 book updates later, regardless of how much wall-clock time passed.
 In busy markets, 10 events is milliseconds. In quiet markets, it could be seconds. 
 This removes the confounding effect of market activity.

Time-horizon labels (later)
y_100ms = mid[t + 100ms] - mid[t]
y_1s    = mid[t + 1s] - mid[t]
Why time-horizon: A signal that is strong at 10 events but dead at 100ms is very different from one that persists at 1s.
